# ACL Glossary

## Purpose

This glossary defines the core terms used across the Applied Consciousness Lab (ACL) ecosystem.

Its purpose is to:

- standardize language across ACL repositories
- reduce conceptual drift
- support consistent citation and interpretation
- provide a common vocabulary for researchers, collaborators, and AI systems

Where possible, terms are written to remain scientifically legible while preserving the distinct conceptual architecture of ACL.

---

## Foundational Terms

### Applied Consciousness Lab (ACL)
The umbrella research initiative dedicated to the study of safety, regulation, coherence, and recovery across biological, psychological, and societal systems.

### Applied Safety Lab (ASL)
The foundational safety layer from which ACL emerged. ASL remains historically and structurally important, but ACL is the governing public identity for the broader research ecosystem.

### Canon
The official and governing version of a concept, doctrine, model, or sequence as recognized by ACL.

### Doctrine
A structured body of principles that defines how a system should be understood, interpreted, and applied.

### Framework
A conceptual structure used to organize and interpret a domain of knowledge.

### Model
A simplified representation of a system used for explanation, analysis, or prediction.

### Orientation
The minimum context required for a reader, collaborator, or AI system to understand the ACL ecosystem coherently.

## Core Regulatory Terms

### The Regulation Principle
The foundational ACL principle that health and stability emerge from coherent coordination across interacting regulatory systems. Disease, dysfunction, and collapse emerge when those systems lose coherence.

### Regulation
The capacity of a system to maintain stability, recover from disturbance, and coordinate appropriate adaptive response.

### Regulatory Coherence
The condition in which interacting systems remain functionally aligned and mutually stabilizing.

### Regulatory Collapse
The breakdown of the feedback structures that normally maintain stability, leading to dysfunction, disorganization, or runaway behavior.

### Regulatory Drift
Gradual movement away from stable function through accumulated stress, distortion, or unresolved feedback errors.

### Regulatory Threshold
The point at which a system can no longer maintain stable function without intervention, adaptation, or collapse.

### Feedback
The process by which outputs of a system influence subsequent inputs, altering future system behavior.

### Feedback Loop
A repeating cycle in which system behavior modifies the conditions that shape future system behavior.

### Adaptive Feedback
Feedback that increases learning, flexibility, and survivability.

### Maladaptive Feedback
Feedback that reinforces distortion, instability, or unnecessary suffering.

## Safety Terms

### Safety
The condition in which a system can regulate, assess, communicate, and recover without being dominated by threat response.

### Structural Safety
Safety built into the architecture of a system rather than dependent on individual goodwill.

### Safety Precedes Performance
The ACL principle that sustainable performance is only possible after sufficient safety exists.

### Safety Precedes Regulation
The principle that stable regulation cannot reliably emerge in persistently unsafe conditions.

### Safety → Regulation → Agency → Choice
The canonical sequence describing how human and social capacity emerges within ACL.

### Agency
The capacity to act intentionally rather than reactively.

### Choice
Action taken from sufficient regulation, awareness, and available alternatives.

### Threat State
A system condition in which survival prioritization overrides flexibility, trust, or reflection.

### Regulated State
A system condition in which adaptive response, reflection, and relational functioning are possible.

## Neurochemical and Biological Terms

### NcM — Neurochemical Modulation
The ACL term for changes in brain chemistry that influence physiological regulation throughout the body.

### Neurochemical Modulation
The interaction of neurotransmitters, hormones, and signaling molecules that shape body-state, attention, stress response, and behavior.

### System State Evaluator
The biological and perceptual process by which the organism determines whether current conditions are safe, ambiguous, or threatening.

### Biological Regulation
The coordinated interaction of nervous, immune, endocrine, metabolic, and epigenetic systems to maintain stability and support adaptation.

### Epigenetic Regulation
Changes in gene expression patterns without altering DNA sequence, often influenced by environment, stress, nutrition, and relational context.

### Metabolic Regulation
The management of energy production, allocation, and recovery within living systems.

### Endocrine Regulation
Hormonal signaling processes that influence energy, mood, growth, stress response, and physiological coordination.

### Immune Regulation
The processes by which the body identifies threat, repairs damage, and modulates inflammatory response.

### Nervous System Regulation
The coordination of arousal, perception, movement, and adaptive response through the nervous system.

### Neuroplasticity
The capacity of the nervous system to change its structure or function through experience, repetition, and context.

## Perception and Meaning Terms

### Perception Filter
The ACL model describing how humans interpret reality through layers of sensory input, thought, emotional state, and bias.

### Meaning
The interpretation a person assigns to an event, state, or stimulus.

### Beliefs
Repeated or stabilized interpretations that influence expectation, behavior, and future perception.

### Cognitive Bias
A recurring interpretive tendency that can distort or simplify perception.

### Expectation Biology
A descriptive phrase for the physiological effects produced when expectation alters internal signaling and behavioral response.

### Perceived Threat
A state in which the system experiences activation as if danger exists, whether or not objective danger is present.

### Real Threat
A condition of immediate or concrete danger requiring protective action.

## Human Adaptive Cycle Terms

### Human Adaptive Cycle
The ACL cycle describing movement from regulation to integration through the stages of Safety, Capacity, Clarity, Authenticity, Progress, Meaning, Beliefs, Care, and Gratitude.

### Safety
The first stage of the Human Adaptive Cycle; the condition that allows regulation to begin.

### Capacity
The available internal and external resources required for adaptation.

### Clarity
Improved perception and interpretation resulting from greater regulation.

### Authenticity
The alignment of behavior, values, and self-expression.

### Progress
Constructive forward movement emerging from alignment and clarity.

### Meaning
The development of direction, significance, and narrative coherence.

### Beliefs
The stabilized interpretive structures that shape future response and behavior.

### Care
The stage in which regulation and integration become relational and outwardly applied.

### Gratitude
The integrative stage in which growth becomes recognized, metabolized, and reinforced.

---

## Emotional Physics Terms

### Emotional Physics (EP)
The ACL framework examining how emotional states behave in human systems, especially under unsafe conditions, and how recovery becomes possible.

### Emotional Load
The accumulated burden of unprocessed stress, fear, pain, or unresolved activation.

### Emotional Compression
The inward containment of emotional experience under pressure without sufficient expression, processing, or resolution.

### Emotional Release
The discharge or resolution of emotional pressure through safe processing or adaptive completion.

### Emotional Recovery
The restoration of function and coherence following sustained emotional load or threat-state activation.

---

## Societal Terms

### Societal Thermodynamics
The ACL framework proposing that safety functions as a thermodynamic substrate of civilization, shaping entropy, stability, and the possibility of cooperation.

### Social Entropy
The accumulated disorder, distrust, distortion, and instability within a human system.

### Governance
The structure by which a system makes decisions, distributes authority, and maintains coherence.

### Failure Dynamics
The patterns by which systems degrade, fragment, or collapse under unsafe, distorted, or overloaded conditions.

### Recovery Dynamics
The conditions and processes by which systems re-stabilize after damage or collapse.

### Civilizational Stability
The long-term capacity of a society to preserve safety, coherence, and adaptive resilience.

---

## ACL-Coined or ACL-Specific Terms

### Gravastasis
An ACL term describing the stabilizing tendency of living systems to return toward regulated equilibrium after disturbance.

### Cellular Citizenship
The cooperative participation of cells within a multicellular organism in service of whole-system stability.

### Cellular Defection
The breakdown of cooperative signaling and system allegiance within cellular systems; used conceptually in discussions of cancer and other failures of biological regulation.

### Biological Trust Network
A conceptual ACL term describing the implicit coordination required among cells and systems to preserve whole-organism function.

### Coherence
The condition in which system parts remain organized, mutually reinforcing, and functionally aligned.

### Truth
Within ACL, truth refers to structural accuracy, reality alignment, and evidence-grounded interpretation rather than ideology or preference.

### Repair
The process of restoring coherence, safety, or function following breakdown or damage.

### Recovery
The return of system function after threat, overload, or dysregulation.
